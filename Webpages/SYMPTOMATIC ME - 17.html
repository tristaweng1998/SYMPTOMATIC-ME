17. Bad Chinese

I want to tell you that my Chinese is bad, or at least its test scores were the worst of all my subjects.

The story is that my Chinese wasn't always bad, actually, it was at the top level when I was in elementary school. But at the beginning of middle school, I got into a big fight with my Chinese teacher because I disagreed with her stringent rules about punishing those who didn't do well in Chinese dictation.

The more detailed story is that I was the class monitor at the time, and I instituted a “class law” where a noticeable but not serious misbehavior should be deducted 2 points from one's “moral evaluation”. Whereas my Chinese teacher insisted that 10 points should be deducted for a failed dictation test, which, in my option, stood for a complete disregard for my regulation. So after that resultless dispute, I refused to prepare myself well for the Chinese test anymore— I saw myself as a martyr — and my grades plummeted.

That accident, in some sense, changed the trajectory of my life. Because it was out of my rejection of Chinese that I fell in love with English, and it was out of my love for English that I began to read and watch stories that were so far away from me then.

Nevertheless, today, as I still speak broken English and feel a tangible regression in my Chinese, I wonder if I would have had a better or more balanced language education if I hadn't met that Chinese teacher. I don't know. Only I sense the sorrow of losing something important.

In April 2022, a bakery chain called Paris Baguette was fined 580,000 yuan (about $90,000) for “illegally” serving bread to residents who were locked inside their homes during the Shanghai lockdown. Some netizens wrote it a couplet that read, "We all suffer bitterness in life, but Paris Baguettes is sweet,” where the Chinese pronunciation of “baguette” is itself similar to “super sweet”. A private joke and a sublime compliment.
