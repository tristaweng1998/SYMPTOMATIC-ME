28. Poetic Idealism

I have always denied being an enthusiast of Chinese culture, because it is a fact that I have never been crazy about it. All I know is what I was taught, or indoctrinated if you prefer to call it.

Nevertheless, today as I am on my way to cultivate some basic yet concrete understanding of the concepts like “freedom, equality and democracy”, which I have known for so long but felt so little, I know that, in all my retrospection and introspection, it is not from them that my irreversible idealism was built up, but precisely from those ancient poems which I was ordered to recite word for word, again and again, in my distant childhood.

One of them reads,
“为天地立心，为生民请命，
为往圣继绝学，为万世开太平。”

And it can be translated as,
“To ordain conscience for Heaven and Earth,
To secure life and fortune for the populace,
To carry on lost teachings of ancient sages,
To establish peace for all future generations.”

And it was written by Zhang Zai, a philosopher and politician who was born exactly one thousand years ago.

These empty cities remind me of another poem,


On the Tower at You Zhou by Chen Zi'ang.


“Where are the great men of the past,

And where are those of future years?

The sky and earth forever last,

Here and now I alone shed tears.”
