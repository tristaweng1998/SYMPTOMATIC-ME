VIII. Wailing wall

Dr. Li, do you know the virus has now been transferred, from the human body to a behemoth? It is a giant behemoth that ignores people because it is too big to see any of them. It only focuses on the road ahead of itself, pretending not to know how many people are trampled to death with each step it takes.

— From Wailing Wall, November 10, 2022

Doctor Li Wenliang (1985-2020)

The letter of admonition issued by the Wuhan Police Bureau ordering Li to stop “spreading rumors” about Covid, was signed by Li,
“I can (stop spreading rumors) and I understand (my misconduct)”

Li Wenliang, an ophthalmologist in the Chinese city of Wuhan, and the “whistle-blower” to the pandemic outbreak, died of the coronavirus on Feb. 6 at the age of 34. More than a month before that, he went online to warn friends of the strange and deadly virus rampaging through his hospital, only to be threatened by government authorities. He became a hero in China when his warnings proved true, then a martyr when he died.

After his passing, people began to gather, virtually, at his last post on Weibo, the Chinese social media platform. In the comments section, they grieve, seek solace, and write down their feelings and thoughts.

Now, with more than a million comments, his last post has become an archive of public memory, or a national diary about the pandemic and the Zero-Covid policy. It chronicles our hidden stories from the very beginning. It is a sanctuary for traumatized people and a place where some truths are preserved.
Also, some call it China’s Wailing Wall, a reference to the Western Wall in Jerusalem, where people leave written prayers in the cracks.
