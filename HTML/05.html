3 The Myth of Given

V. The Myth of Given

In my philosophy studies, one of the intriguing but no-ending debates I have encountered is about the “The Myth of Given”, which illuminates the difficulty of articulating the relationship between the nature of mind and of perception. Simply put, it asks, “Do I really see what I see?”, where the former "see" implies both the consequence of seeing as action and the insight of seeing as knowledge-induced understanding.

But is our “given” limited to the domain of our perception?

I held this question to myself and gradually came to see that “The Myth of Given” is more ethical than epistemological. It sheds light, and at the same time casts doubt, on everything we are born with, such as race, gender and social class, that constitutes our “first identity”.

Then the question turns to something that demands our positive response, which is, “Should I see myself as I am seen?”

The White Guards, a nod to the Red Guards during the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), have been a ubiquitous feature of China’s Zero-Covid Policy. Composed of police officers, medical personnel and members of neighborhood committees, they serve as a major part of the state apparatus to ensure the people’s obedience to the government. Disguising their true identities with suits, they have earned themselves a reputation for physical violence and abuse of power.
